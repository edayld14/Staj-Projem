The Blue Mosque

The Sultan Ahmed Mosque, also known as the Blue Mosque, was commissioned by Ottoman Sultan Ahmed I and built by Architect Sedefkar Mehmed Agha between 1609 and 1617 on the historic peninsula of Istanbul. The mosque is called the "Blue Mosque" by Europeans because of its decoration with blue, green, and white İznik tiles and the blue-themed calligraphic work inside its large dome and semi-domes. After the Hagia Sophia was converted into a museum in 1935, the Sultan Ahmed Mosque became Istanbul's principal mosque.

In fact, the Sultan Ahmed Mosque complex is one of the largest structures in Istanbul. This complex includes a mosque, madrasahs, a sultan's palace, an arcade, shops, a bathhouse, a fountain, water dispensers, a tomb, a darüşşifa (hospital), a primary school, a soup kitchen, and rental rooms. Some of these structures have not survived to the present day.

Architecturally and artistically, the most noteworthy aspect of the structure is its decoration with over 20,000 İznik tiles. The tiles feature traditional plant motifs in yellow and blue tones, which elevate the building beyond just a place of worship. The prayer hall of the mosque measures 64 by 72 meters. The central dome, which rises to a height of 43 meters, has a diameter of 23.5 meters. The interior of the mosque is illuminated by over 200 colored glass windows. The inscriptions inside were written by Seyyid Kasım Gubari from Diyarbakır. The mosque forms part of a complex and is the first mosque in Turkey with six minarets.
