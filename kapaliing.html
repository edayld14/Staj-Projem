Grand Bazaar

The Grand Bazaar, located in the center of Istanbul, between the districts of Beyazıt, Nuruosmaniye, and Mercan, is one of the largest and oldest covered markets in the world. The Grand Bazaar has approximately 4,000 shops, with around 25,000 employees working there. It is said that during its busiest times, it can host up to 500,000 people. In 2014, it was ranked as the most visited tourist attraction in the world with 91,250,000 visitors. The Grand Bazaar is often referred to as the world's first shopping mall.

The core of the Grand Bazaar consists of two bedestens (vaulted warehouses). The İç Bedesten (Inner Bedesten), also known as the Cevahir Bedesteni, is believed to be a structure from the Byzantine era, measuring 48 m x 36 m. The Yeni Bedesten (New Bedesten), constructed by Sultan Mehmed the Conqueror in 1460, is the second significant structure of the Grand Bazaar and is known as the Sandal Bedesteni. It is named after the "sandal" fabric sold there, which was woven with both cotton and silk.

The year 1460, when the construction of the Grand Bazaar began, is accepted as its foundation year. The main large bazaar was constructed in wood by Sultan Suleiman the Magnificent.

With 66 streets and around 4,000 shops spread over 30,700 square meters, the Grand Bazaar is a unique and must-see center in Istanbul. Resembling a city with its fully covered site, it has developed and expanded over time. Until recent times, it housed 5 mosques, 1 school, 7 fountains, 10 wells, 1 sebil (public water fountain), 1 şadırvan (ablution fountain), 24 gates, and 17 hans (inns).

Surrounding the two old buildings from the 15th century, with their thick walls and series of domes, streets have been covered and extended over the centuries to form a shopping center. In the past, each street housed specific professions with strict regulation of handmade production (manufacture), high respect for commercial ethics and traditions. Every type of valuable fabric, jewelry, weapon, and antique item was sold by families specialized for generations, ensuring complete trust. Although the Grand Bazaar was restored to its former state after the earthquake and several major fires at the end of the last century, its characteristics have undergone changes.

All shops were constructed to have the same width. In each street, master craftsmen of different products formed guilds (e.g., quilt makers, slipper makers). Competition among sellers was strictly forbidden. A master could not place his workbench outside the shop to attract the crowd, and prices could not exceed those set by the state. Some walls and a dome of the bazaar collapsed during the 1894 Istanbul earthquake.
