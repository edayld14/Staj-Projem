Galata Tower

The Galata Tower was built by the Genoese in the mid-14th century, during the period when the Galata district was a Genoese colony, as part of the Galata city walls. During the Ottoman period, it was used as a prison for a time before being converted into a fire watchtower. The tower, which was damaged by earthquakes and fires from time to time, underwent several repairs during the Ottoman period. By the 1960s, the tower was in a rather dilapidated state, but it was restored by the Istanbul Municipality and opened to visitors. In 2020, the Galata Tower was restored again by the General Directorate of Foundations, with the removal of later added concrete elements and the cafeteria, and it was reopened to visitors with a museum function.

In the Galata Tower Museum, artifacts reflecting all periods of Istanbul, which has been a stage for settlement since prehistoric times and served as the capital of three major empires for about sixteen centuries, are displayed. The top floor of the Galata Tower, which was one of the tallest structures in Istanbul until the mid-20th century, offers one of the most beautiful panoramic views of the city. The tower, along with the Genoese towers in the Mediterranean and Black Sea, is listed as a tentative World Heritage site by UNESCO.

While You're Here
You can walk to the Galata Mevlevihanesi, the first Mevlevi lodge in Istanbul and the most important Ottoman structure in Beyoğlu. Additionally, if you head up Istiklal Street from the Tower, the Tarık Tunaya Cultural Center is also open for free visits.
