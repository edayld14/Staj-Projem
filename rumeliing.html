Rumeli Hisarı

Rumeli Fortress (also known as Boğazkesen Fortress) is located in the Sarıyer district of Istanbul, on the Bosphorus, and is named after the neighborhood it is situated in. It was constructed by Fatih Sultan Mehmet before the conquest of Istanbul to prevent attacks coming from the Black Sea, built opposite the Anadolu Fortress on the Anatolian side of the Bosphorus at its narrowest point (698 meters).

The fortress covers an area of 30 acres. Its three large towers, completed in just ninety days, feature some of the largest castle towers in the world. The name Rumeli Fortress appears in various historical records: in Fatih’s waqfiyah as Kulle-i Cedide; in Neşri’s history as Yenice Hisar; and in the chronicles of Kemalpaşazade, Aşıkpaşazade, and Nişancı as Boğazkesen Hisarı.

For many years, Rumeli Fortress hosted Rumeli Hisarı Concerts.

The construction of the fortress began on April 15, 1452. The work was divided among various pashas, with each overseeing a section; Fatih Sultan Mehmet personally supervised the section facing the sea. Saruca Paşa was responsible for the right tower when viewed from the sea, Zağanos Paşa for the left tower, and Halil Paşa for the coastal tower. The towers bear the names of these pashas. The construction of the fortress was completed on August 31, 1452.

Rumeli Fortress has three major towers named Saruca Paşa, Halil Paşa, and Zağanos Paşa, along with the Small Zağanos Paşa and 13 variously sized bastions. Saruca Paşa and Halil Paşa towers are nine stories high, including their ground floors, while Zağanos Paşa Tower is eight stories high. The diameter of Saruca Paşa Tower is 23.30 meters, with a wall thickness of 7 meters and a height of 28 meters. Zağanos Paşa Tower has a diameter of 26.70 meters, a wall thickness of 5.70 meters, and a height of 21 meters. Halil Paşa Tower has a diameter of 23.30 meters, a wall thickness of 6.5 meters, and a height of 22 meters.

Rumeli Fortress was severely damaged in the Great Istanbul Earthquake of 1509 but was immediately repaired. In the fire of 1746, the wooden parts were destroyed. The fortress was later repaired during the reign of Sultan Selim III (1789-1807). When the wooden domes covering the towers collapsed, the fortress was filled with small wooden houses. In 1953, under the instruction of President Celâl Bayar, three Turkish female architects, Cahide Tamer Aksel, Selma Emler, and Mualla Eyüboğlu Anhegger, initiated the necessary restoration work. The wooden houses within the fortress were expropriated and demolished, and the restoration was carried out.
