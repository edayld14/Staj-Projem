Gülhane Park

Gülhane Park was the outer garden of Topkapı Palace during the Ottoman Empire, housing a grove and rose gardens. The Tanzimat Edict, the first concrete step towards democratization in Turkish history, was read in Gülhane Park on November 3, 1839, by Foreign Minister Mustafa Reşit Pasha during the reign of Abdülmecid, and is also referred to as the Gülhane Hatt-ı Hümayun.

The park was reorganized and opened to the public in 1912 during the tenure of Istanbul Mayor Cemil Pasha (Topuzlu). The total area of the park is about 163 acres. At the entrance to the park, on the right side, there are busts of Istanbul mayors. A tree-lined path runs through the middle of the park. On either side of this path are resting areas and a children's playground. Just to the right of the slope that winds down towards the Bosphorus, there is a statue of Aşık Veysel, and near the bottom of the slope, there is the Roman-era Goths Column.

The Sarayburnu Park section was once connected to the main park by a bridge over the Sirkeci Railway line. This part was later separated from the park by the coastal road (1958). The Sarayburnu section contains the first statue of Atatürk erected after the Republic (October 3, 1926). The statue was made by the Australian architect Kripel. Atatürk demonstrated the Latin alphabet to the public for the first time in this park on September 1, 1928. The last ceremony in Istanbul for Atatürk's body before it was sent to Ankara was held in the Sarayburnu section of Gülhane Park on November 19, 1938. The coffin was taken from the gun carriage by 12 generals and placed on the Zafer destroyer, which was docked at the pier to be transported to the Yavuz battleship.
