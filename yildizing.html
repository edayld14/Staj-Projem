Yıldız Palace

Yıldız Palace, originally built for Sultan Selim III’s (1789-1807) mother Mihrişah Sultan, was primarily used as the main palace of the Ottoman Empire during the reign of Sultan Abdul Hamid II (1876-1909). Today, it is located in the Beşiktaş district. Unlike the Dolmabahçe Palace, it is not a single structure but a complex of palaces, pavilions, administrative, security, service buildings, and parks situated within a garden and woodland that stretches from the Marmara Sea coast northwestward up to the ridge.

This area has been used as a hunting ground for sultans since the time of Suleiman the Magnificent (1520-1566). Although it is not certain how much of the palace grounds overlap, gardens and woodlands named "Civan Kapucıbaşı Garden" and "Kazancıoğlu Garden" likely included the Yıldız Palace grounds. These gardens were incorporated into the sultan’s gardens during the reign of Ahmed I (1603-1617).

Subsequently, many structures were added to the area at various times as needed. These meticulously constructed buildings turned the area into a living space.

It is said that Abdul Hamid II abandoned the Dolmabahçe Palace, which had witnessed two revolutions, due to emotional reasons and moved to the more secure Yıldız Palace in 1876. During this period, Yıldız became the focal point of political administration, overshadowing Bab-ı Ali, which had been the center of political life during the Tanzimat period. In 1882, the palace court that sentenced Mithat Pasha and Mahmud Celaleddin Pasha to death was held at Yıldız Palace, hence gaining the name Yıldız Court. After this event, Yıldız Palace became notorious as a center of fear and intrigue under Abdul Hamid II’s rule, and at one point, the use of the word "star" in the Ottoman press was banned by his censorship administration due to its political connotations. Following Abdul Hamid’s deposition after the 31 March Incident in 1909, the palace was plundered and partially burned by a mob. It is said that during this looting, individuals who had submitted reports to Abdul Hamid or worked as police agents tried to find and destroy documents pertaining to them.

Restoration work that began in 2019 was completed in 2024.
