Küçüksu Pavilion

The meadow area between the Göksu and Küçüksu streams was one of the sultans' favorite gardens on the Bosphorus during the Ottoman period and became one of the most popular recreational spots over time.

In the 17th century, the famous traveler Evliya Çelebi described Göksu, which he referred to as "a river of life," as a tranquil place surrounded by rose gardens, small pavilions, and state-owned mills, with boats rowing on the river. Sultan Murad IV (1623-1640) had the area around Küçüksu and the densely planted cypress trees up to Kandilli organized and named it "Gümüş Selvi" (Silver Cypress).

The first construction within the Hasbahçe (Garden) began during the reign of Sultan Mahmud I (1730-1754). Sadrazam Divitdar Mehmed Emin Pasha commissioned a wooden pavilion for the Sultan, who frequently hunted and practiced shooting at Göksu. This two-story structure by the sea underwent major renovations during the reign of Sultan Selim III (1789-1807), and at the Sultan's request, a fountain was added in 1806 in honor of his beloved mother, Mihrişah Valide Sultan. The old pavilion, which continued to be used during the reign of Sultan Mahmud II (1808-1839), was demolished by Sultan Abdülmecid (1839-1861) and replaced with the new Küçüksu Pavilion, built between 1856 and 1857. During the reign of Sultan Abdülaziz (1861-1876), the façade decorations of the pavilion were enriched.

The Küçüksu Pavilion was opened as a museum-palace in 1983.

Consisting of three floors, including the basement, the Küçüksu Pavilion is constructed on a 15x27 meter area using traditional masonry techniques. The basement is allocated for storage, kitchen, and staff, while the upper floors are arranged into four rooms opening onto a central space. This structure reflects the traditional Turkish house plan and serves as a "riding pavilion" generally used for relaxation and hunting.

The façade, decorated with reliefs and featuring a small pool with a fountain, and the staircase, incorporate Western decorative motifs. The ceiling decorations include plaster reliefs and pencil work, and the fireplaces are made of valuable Italian marbles in various colors and styles. Each room is adorned with different decorations, fine craftsmanship parquet floors, European-style furniture, carpets, and paintings, giving the pavilion the appearance of a rich art museum.
