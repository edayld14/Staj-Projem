Arasta Bazaar

It is also called the Mirrored Passage due to the mirrors inside.

The building was constructed in 1874 by Armenian merchant Onnik Düz and architect Pulgher. Before the European Passage and the nearby Pera Site, the Naum Theatre and the Jardin des Fleurs Hotel were located there. However, these buildings, along with many others, were destroyed in the Great Beyoğlu Fire on June 5, 1870. As a result, the current European Passage was built in the area.

Initially, the passage was illuminated by gas lamps placed in front of the mirrors. In the early years of the building, it housed a shoemaker, two barbers, two tailors, a grocery store, a dry goods store, two soap makers, and a rug dealer. In later years, businesses such as florists, watchmakers, piano manufacturers, and fashion houses operated in the passage.

Today, the passage houses souvenir shops selling items like Yemeni, postcards, and ceramics. It is also possible to find rug dealers and antique shops.

The neoclassical-style passage, which is 56 meters long, contains 22 shops. The building features fire-resistant Maltese stone. Each shop has its own staff room, kitchen, and basement and also includes two additional floors. Large sculptures adorn the upper facades of the shops facing the passage. The passage’s ceiling is partially covered with windows. The building also features chandeliers hanging from the ceiling for illumination. The front faces of the shops are covered with glass down to the ground. Arches are located between each shop.
