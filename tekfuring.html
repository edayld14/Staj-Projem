Palace of the Porphyrogenitus

The Imperial House Used by the Emperors During the Glorious Period of Byzantium
The imperial palace used by Byzantine emperors from the 12th century onwards was part of the Blachernai palace complex and remained in existence for a long time. It is not precisely known who constructed the palace or when it was built. In the 16th century, Europeans referred to the building as the Constantinian Palace (Palatium Constantini) and later as the Porphyrogenitus Palace. The historic building, located in a commanding position and under the protection of the city's ramparts, was also referred to in ancient sources as a “High Palace.”

The Tekfur Palace was one of the palaces used by emperors during the last glorious period of Byzantine palaces. It was ideally located to protect the emperors against potential uprisings. During the 57 years of Latin occupation of Istanbul, almost all of the Tekfur Palace and other imperial structures around it were burned and destroyed. Even the lead from the roofs of the buildings was melted down and sold to Venetian merchants.

The First Imperial Building Captured During the Conquest
The Tekfur Palace, where the emperor resided, was among the primary targets during the conquest of Istanbul. It became historically known as the first imperial building captured by the invading troops. The palace witnessed the fiercest battles of the conquest on the ground between Edirnekapı and Eğrikapı. The palace was later repaired and used for various purposes.

Depicted with its roof in Piri Reis’s drawing, the Tekfur Palace turned into ruins again in the 17th century. Some parts were used as an elephant stable and a zoo. Later, the palace was transformed into a glass and tile workshop and gained fame for its tiles. Many mosques preferred “Tekfur Palace tiles” in their construction. These tiles, which became a brand in their own right, adorned historical structures such as the Sultan Ahmed III Fountain and Hekimoğlu Ali Paşa Mosque.
