Dikilitaş

The Obelisk was originally erected by the Egyptian Pharaoh Thutmose III in the 15th century BC and placed to the south of the seventh pylon of the Karnak temple. In 357 AD, Roman Emperor Constantius II had the obelisk transported to Alexandria to celebrate the 20th year of his reign. Later, in 390 AD, Emperor Theodosius I had it transported by ship to Constantinople (now Istanbul) and erected it in its current location at the Hippodrome.

The Obelisk was made from red Aswan granite and originally stood 30 meters tall. However, due to damage either during transportation or while being placed in its current location, its present height is 18.45 meters (24.87 meters with its base). It weighs approximately 200 tons.

Lady Montagu, in one of her letters dated 1718 from her book "The Turkish Embassy Letters," wrote the following: This stone is placed on a pedestal made of square-cut stone and stands on four columns. There are reliefs of a battle and a council on two sides of the stone. The other two sides bear the following inscriptions in Greek and Latin:

Northwest Side
“Thutmose III of the 18th Dynasty, ruler of Upper and Lower Egypt, after offering his sacrifice to the god Amon, with the help of Horus, brought all seas and rivers under his dominion and erected this column during the 30th year of his reign, hoping for many more celebrations to come.”

North Side
“After offering his sacrifice to the god Amon, who embodies every manifestation of the sacred and hidden name, in great humility and seeking his assistance, the powerful ruler of Upper and Lower Egypt, the light of religion, the friend of the south, set out to extend the borders of his kingdom to Mesopotamia.”

Southeast Side
“The pharaoh, chosen by the Sun himself, who possesses the gold colors spread to the world at sunrise, made this work for his father Ra, bearing the strength, wealth, love, and respect given by Horus and holding the crown of Upper and Lower Egypt.”

South Side
“The pharaoh, favored by the god Horus and bearing the title of Son of the Sun, ruler of Upper and Lower Egypt, spread light with power and justice to all horizons. He led his army, sailed the Mediterranean, conquered the whole world, and extended his borders to Naharin. He marched determinedly to Mesopotamia and fought great battles.”
